What is Geometry?

Geometry is the study of shapes, lines, and space. It starts with three basic ideas — points, lines, and planes — which we accept without formal definition. A point represents a location, a line extends infinitely in both directions, and a plane is a flat surface stretching forever. Using proper notation and clear labeling is key to avoiding mistakes.


Segments and Midpoints

A segment is a finite piece of a line with two endpoints. If point B lies between points A and C, the Segment Addition Postulate tells us AB + BC = AC. Congruent segments have equal lengths (≅), and a segment bisector passes through the midpoint of a segment.


Distance Between Points

The distance formula calculates how far apart two points are:
d = √((x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²)
The midpoint formula finds the center of a segment:
M = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2)


Angles and Their Relationships

Angles are formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint (vertex). Angles can be acute, right, obtuse, or straight. The Angle Addition Postulate states that if a point lies inside an angle, the sum of the two smaller angles equals the whole angle. Understanding complementary, supplementary, vertical, and linear pair angles helps solve problems and proofs.


Key Takeaways

  • Label diagrams clearly: points, lines, planes, segments, and angles.
  • Use the Segment Addition Postulate: AB + BC = AC when B is between A and C.
  • Distance and midpoint formulas allow calculation of lengths and centers of segments.
  • Angles can be classified and added using the Angle Addition Postulate.
  • Vertical and linear pair angles have predictable relationships useful in proofs.
